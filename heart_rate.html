Heart Rate & Excercise

Your heart rate is the literal speed of your heartbeat. Heart rate is measured by counting the number of contractions your heart preforms within one minute (hence a measurement of "Beats Per Minute (BPM)") A heart rate above 100 bpm (resting) is considered high, this is often referred to as "Tachycardia" while a heart rate below 60 bpm (resting) is considered to be too low and is often referred to as "Bradycardia"

The heart is a muscular organ, and like any muscle it requires excerise and delicate care. Too much immediate strain on the heart can lead to numerous acute problems such as a heart attack, long term strain or inactivity can lead to more chronic illnesses such as heart disease. Apart from physical excersie there are several other influences that impact heart rate, ranging from thyroid hormones to the ingestion of stimulants and depressants succh as caffine and alcohol.

Heart Rate Zone Benefit Example Activities
Vigorous (greater than 90% of Maximum Heart Rate) Excellent for atheletic high-intensity training & visceral fat reduction Sprinting, Spin biking, fast pace swim, high-intesity treadmil
Intense (between 80% to 90% of Maximum Heart Rate) Excellent for interval training & visceral fat reduction Track workouts, medium pace spin biking, medium pace swim
Moderate (between 70% and 79% of Maximum Heart Rate) Best pace for a steady and sustainable work out of 30 - 40 minutes Jogging, casual swim sprints, average treadmil walk
Light (between 60% and 69% of Maximum Heart Rate) Perfect "transition" pace to gear up into a higher heart zone or to gear down towards the end of your workout Fast pace walk or walking a dog
Easy (between 50% and 59% of Maximum Heart Rate) Ideal zone for pr-workout activity should be engaged for 15 - 20 minutes Casual walking, walking and talking, stretching

This calculator tools helps you determine your "Maximum Heart Rate" and should be used in conjuntion with the table to help you get an idea of what kind of workout you should do based off of the general benefits that particular workout has. Think of the benefits in relation to your personal health goals and keep a general check on your heart rate using a smart watch or heart rate counter while working out to get a better idea of how you're doing. Start of at the bottom of the heart rate zones and progressively work your way up, peaking at the vigorous activity zone if you are interested in a high intensity work out, from there taiper back down until you reach the easy zone to cool down
